The problem

Ten interpretations of one bank

Products were shipping. Interfaces followed brand guidelines. Teams were moving. But the experience didn't behave like one bank — there were ten different interpretations of what the bank is.

The real issue
Design was reactive — "what's good enough to ship?" — instead of "what is the right experience for the customer?"
Root cause
The issue wasn't design talent. It was the absence of a system to define and enforce quality.

The shift

From support function to governance system

I reframed UX from a design support function into a governance system for product quality. The goal: make experience quality consistent, measurable, and enforceable across the bank.

What I built

A 3-layer UX foundation

Three interlocking layers connecting directly to how products are defined, designed, built, and released.

1
Principles — aligning how the bank behaves
Behavioral principles establishing a shared language. Designed for financial confidence, not just task completion.
Clarity over clevernessNo hidden consequencesOne truth across channelsMistakes don't become disasters
2
Quality standards — defining "good" in measurable terms
Enforceable standards that define minimum acceptable experience before release. Removed subjectivity entirely.
≥90% task success≤60s for common actions≤4 steps high-frequencyFull cost before confirm
3
Scorecard — making quality visible
A shared, objective language between design, product, and leadership. 1–5 per category, total score out of 45.
Task successClarityEfficiencyTransparencyTrustConsistencyAccessibilityPerformance
Design principle
Teams could no longer say "this looks fine." They had to prove "this meets the standard."
