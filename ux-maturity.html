Chapter 1

The question that started it all

In late 2025, the design organization at TBC Bank started a conversation that most banks never have: where do we actually stand in User Experience? Not by instinct, not by perception — but by evidence.

We ran an initial survey across the organization to capture the team's perception of our maturity level across key experience dimensions. The framework we used was grounded in the Nielsen Norman Group's 6-stage UX maturity model — a globally recognized scale from Absent to User-driven.

Nielsen Norman Group's 6-stage UX maturity model

Survey result — perceived level
4
Structured — partly systematic, variably effective. The team believed we had process, but inconsistently applied.
What level 4 means
Structured
UX is partly systematic. Some evidence-driven decisions. Process exists but is not universal — effectiveness varies by team and product area.
Perception is a starting point, not a measurement. A score of 4 felt right in the room — but feeling right and being right are different things. That gap is exactly what this project was built to close.

The framework

Six stages. One direction.

The maturity scale runs from 1 to 6. Each stage describes how embedded, systematic, and business-connected UX is within the organization. The goal is not to reach 6 overnight — it's to know exactly where you are and move deliberately.

Chapter 2

Building the measurement system

Perception is a starting point. We needed data. So we kicked off a structured project to define what "measurable UX maturity" actually looks like at TBC Bank.

The work resulted in a set of concrete, trackable data points organized across 7 business-relevant dimensions — from customer experience quality to accessibility. Each dimension scores 1–6. Together they produce a single, honest maturity score.

25+ metrics defined across 7 categories

Important caveat. The data we have is real — but not yet universal. We do not have full coverage across every experience flow and product surface. We are actively working with Product and Digital teams to ensure tracking is in place everywhere it needs to be. Until then, the score reflects a partial view, and should be read as directional rather than definitive.

What's next

By end of Q2 2026 we are adding Research & Validation and Collaboration & Culture — the categories that tell us whether we build the right things and whether design is truly embedded across the organization. Each new category makes the score more precise and more meaningful.

Research & validation

Are we validating before we build? Prototype testing rates, insights adoption, post-release checks.

Collaboration & culture

Is design embedded in the process? Backlog involvement, stakeholder participation, feedback loops.

The maturity score does not exist to grade the design team. It exists to give the organization a shared, honest view of where customer experience investment is paying off — and where it isn't. When we know our score, we know where to focus.
"We didn't start this to prove the design team's value. We started it because TBC's customers deserve an organization that knows — with data — whether it is getting better at serving them."
