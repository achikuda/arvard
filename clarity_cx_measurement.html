Portfolio case study · TBC Bank · 2025–2026
The measurement
that showed the truth
CX metrics across 146 journeys were built to confirm success, not find it. This is how we reset them — and what happened when reality finally showed up on the dashboard.
Chief Design Officer
146 journeys · ~2,000 triggers
Scores dropped. Good.
01 · The problem
The dashboard said great.
The experience was not.
TBC Bank had Medallia. Product teams had NPS. Nobody had accurate signal.

NPS placed after button taps

Product teams triggered surveys at the end of successful transactions — measuring whether a UI function worked, not whether the bank earned trust.

CSAT after single interactions

Feedback collected immediately after one touchpoint. No account for what came before, or what happened after disbursement.

CES at the wrong moments

Effort scores placed where flows ended — not where customers struggled. High-friction steps and channel transitions were invisible.

Ownership was decentralized

Each product team instrumented surveys independently. No framework. No consistency. No ability to compare signal across journeys.

Root cause
Customers were answering "did this button work?" — and the answer was always yes. So scores stayed high, confidence stayed false, and real problems went undetected.
02 · The reset
Each metric redefined
from first principles.
NPS, CSAT, and CES each measure something distinct. We rebuilt the logic behind all three.
NPS Measures trust — not UI satisfaction

Loan approval/rejection · financing decisions · moments where the bank delivers or breaks a promise

"Would you recommend the bank?" — not the feature. Not the screen. The bank.

CSAT Measures end-to-end product experience

Loan disbursed + repayment started · multiple touchpoints engaged · post-sale experience included

From single interaction to full journey. If the bank let you down at step 4, step 1's score doesn't matter.

CES Measures friction — where customers struggle

High-effort steps · channel transitions (digital → branch) · known drop-off points

Where customers struggle, not where flows end. The hard part, not the confirmation screen.

03 · Execution
Not a framework update.
A full re-instrumentation.
Every journey. Every trigger. Every ownership line.
146
journeys mapped and redefined
~2K
survey triggers rebuilt from scratch
1
owner — CX, not product teams improvising
Structural change
Survey ownership moved from product teams acting independently to a single CX function with consistent logic across all products — making cross-journey comparison possible for the first time.
04 · What happened
By March, scores dropped sharply.
That was the intended outcome.
Before
High scores
False positives. Metrics reflecting whether UI functioned — not whether customers trusted the bank or felt their needs were met.
After
Real signal
Actual customer sentiment. Teams could finally see where trust broke, where effort was high, and what to prioritize — by impact, not opinion.
The principle
A score that only goes up is not a metric — it's a mirror for the team that built it. The first honest score is the most valuable score you'll ever see.
05 · Closing the loop
Collecting feedback without acting
on it is expensive decoration.
We added a callback team to turn signal into recovery.
Detractors

Understand and recover trust

Every detractor reached directly. The goal isn't to dispute the score — it's to understand what broke and show the bank is paying attention.

Neutrals

Fastest path to promoters

Neutrals already have a relationship. A single meaningful interaction — acknowledging friction, offering resolution — is often enough to move them.

System closed
Detect signal → understand the cause → validate → act → measure again. Feedback is not a report. It's the start of a conversation.
The most important thing we shipped in Year 1 wasn't a feature. It was the ability to see clearly.
