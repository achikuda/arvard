1The problem

A design system existed.
Nobody used it.

When I joined TBC Bank, the system had 41 components and ~15% adoption. Teams were faster building from scratch than waiting on platform approvals.
Component library

Root cause
Teams weren't ignoring the system. The system wasn't worth using. No composite patterns, no contribution path, no adoption visibility — just a library of atomic parts nobody had time to assemble.
2The gap

Four structural
problems.

The audit revealed it wasn't a component coverage problem. It was a system design problem.
01
Missing composites
Teams rebuilt list systems, forms, and cards from scratch. Patterns weren't codified anywhere.
02
No native-first principle
Custom UI replaced native iOS and Android components. Extra build cost, ongoing maintenance.
03
No contribution model
Platform was the only path. Every change needed approval. Review cycles took days or weeks.
04
No adoption tracking
Nobody knew what was being used, extended, or ignored. No visibility, no accountability.
3The solution

Audit. Principle.
Composites. Governance.

Four moves in sequence. Each one removed a blocker the previous one exposed.
Phase 01
Full audit
Mapped every component across iOS and Android. Catalogued gaps, duplicates, deviation levels. Identified where native could replace custom.
Phase 02
Native-first principle
Use native platform components where possible. Customize only where it creates real product value. Teams identified swap candidates immediately.
Phase 03
Composite components
List system, form modules, cards, navigation patterns — fully assembled patterns, not raw atoms. Ready to configure and ship.
Phase 04
Contribution model
Reuse / extend / create tiers. No central gatekeeper. Teams contribute without waiting. Long review cycles — eliminated.
Key insight
Also replaced the custom print-optimized brand font with a system-optimized one — better readability across devices, lower rendering overhead, easier for engineering to implement consistently.
4Intelligence

Atoms weren't the
problem. Molecules were.

Teams had parts. They kept spending time assembling them into the same patterns — every sprint, every team. Composite components ended that.
List system Icons, metadata, swipe/tap actions, states. Eliminated rebuilds across transactions, beneficiaries, offers.
Form modules Validation, errors, helper text, multi-step flows. Fixed inconsistency in onboarding, loans, payments.
Card components Account cards, loan summaries, status indicators — content + actions + state in one unit.
Navigation Headers, tab systems, contextual action bars — aligned across platforms.
The shift
Before composites, teams assembled the same patterns from scratch — every sprint.
After: pick a composite, configure it, ship. Composites drove ~60% of the efficiency gains.
Result
Teams shifted from rebuilding UI to focusing on product logic and customer value.
5Contribution model

Three tiers.
No bottlenecks.

Defined when teams reuse, extend, or create. Removed the dependency on centralized approvals and eliminated long review cycles between product and platform teams.
Reuse
Component exists. Use it as-is. No review needed. Full velocity — the default path for the majority of work.
Extend
Component exists but needs variation. Extend with lightweight documentation. Light review. Pattern becomes available for others.
Create
Pattern doesn't exist anywhere. Build it, document it, contribute it back. Becomes the new system standard.
Impact
Platform team freed from approval burden — shifted focus to system quality and coverage. Product teams gained autonomy without sacrificing consistency.

7Lessons

What I'd tell the
next DS lead.

01
Atoms without molecules are a library, not a system.
Composite components delivered more adoption than any foundation work. Teams need patterns, not parts.
02
Gatekeeping kills adoption faster than missing components.
Removing centralized approval was the unlock. Speed of contribution drives quality of coverage.
03
Adoption only moves if it's tracked.
Visibility created accountability. Once teams could see usage, the conversation shifted from "should we use it" to "how far are we behind."
04
Native-first is a business argument, not a design preference.
Custom components carry ongoing maintenance debt. Every native replacement freed engineering cycles for product work.
The most important thing we did wasn't add more components. It was make the system worth using — and then make sure everyone knew how.
