TBC Bank · Navigation Redesign Proposal
Unifying Transfers, Payment & Products
into a coherent navigation model
Today the app splits one user job — moving money — across two separate tabs, and mixes financial assets with payment tools in a third. This proposal consolidates into a model where every screen has one clear job, and the user never has to know which tab owns which action.
What we're optimizing against
Task completion without friction
A user should be able to initiate any money movement from a single surface without knowing the bank's internal product taxonomy.
Financial confidence, not just task completion
Users must understand their full financial position — assets, liabilities, upcoming obligations — without switching between screens.
Cross-channel consistency
The mental model a user builds on mobile should translate cleanly to web and branch — one taxonomy everywhere.
Product metrics affected
Transfer & payment task success rate ↑
Fewer wrong-tab navigations. Users find the right action on first attempt. Target: ≥90% task success per CX standards.
Steps to complete frequent actions ↓
Consolidation removes one navigation step for every bill payment. Target: ≤4 steps per CX quality standard.
Offers tab open rate ↑
Contextual, data-anchored offers replace generic promotions. Users trust the tab because every card is relevant to their account.
Support contacts for navigation errors ↓
Clearer information hierarchy reduces "where do I find X" calls. Direct cost reduction measurable via Medallia + call center data.
Problems on screen → TBC Experience Design Principles violated
Transfers + Payment split
Principle violated
Clarity over cleverness — the user must know which tab owns which destination before they can act. The interface requires domain knowledge the user doesn't have.
Also violates
Effortless frequent actions — a daily-use flow (pay a bill) requires navigating to a separate tab from a recent transfer.
Payment templates show $0.00
Principle violated
No surprises — $0.00 on every saved template reads as either nothing owed or a system error. The user cannot tell if the template is working before tapping it.
Also violates
Financial transparency — no amount, no last paid date, no due date visible before interaction.
Products mixes assets, liabilities & services
Principle violated
Financial confidence, not just task completion — a screen that shows a $18k balance next to a fines monitoring service gives no sense of financial position. There is no hierarchy between what you own and what you owe.
Also violates
Cross-channel consistency — the taxonomy on this screen does not map to how a branch advisor would describe the user's financial portfolio.
No Home / briefing screen
Principle violated
Financial confidence, not just task completion — the user has no single surface that answers "where do I stand today?" They must visit three separate tabs to piece together their net position, upcoming bills, and recent activity.
Also violates
Trust over speed — without visibility into upcoming obligations, users are surprised by payments they forgot to make.
Frequent transfers have no amount or date
Principle violated
Clarity over cleverness — showing a name and masked IBAN without the last amount or date forces the user to tap into the flow before they can confirm it's the right recipient and right amount.
Also violates
Error prevention — no contextual data at the list level means the user cannot catch a wrong selection before initiating.
Offers is a billboard not a service
Principle violated
Contextual offers — the principle explicitly requires offers to be relevant to the user's financial behaviour. Generic promotions not anchored to account data violate this directly.
Also violates
Trust over speed — an Offers tab perceived as advertising erodes trust in the bank's intent, reducing engagement across the whole app.

The real problem
8,600 users open Transfers every day and walk past 800 payable services they never knew were there.
The Payment tab is effectively invisible to the majority of daily active users. Not because they don't need it — because the navigation trains them to ignore it.

This model earns the user's attention with three types of content only: something you qualify for (with the exact dollar benefit calculated from your data), something that would save you money (before/after comparison shown), and rate or policy changes that affect your existing products.
